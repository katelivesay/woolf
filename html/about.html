About The Author

Virginia Woolf (1882-1941) was a British writer and essayist who is considered one of the most influential modernist authors of the 20th century. She is known for her groundbreaking novels, such as "Mrs. Dalloway," "To the Lighthouse," and "Orlando," as well as for her literary essays and feminist writings.

Born into an upper-middle-class family in London, Woolf was educated at home by her parents and later attended King's College London. In 1912, she married Leonard Woolf, and together they founded the Hogarth Press, which published works by many modernist writers, including T.S. Eliot and E.M. Forster.

Woolf's writing is characterized by her use of stream of consciousness narration and her exploration of the inner lives of her characters. Her novels often deal with themes of gender and sexuality, mental illness, and the effects of social and historical change on individuals and society.

In addition to her fiction writing, Woolf was also a prolific essayist and literary critic, and her essays on writing, feminism, and literary history continue to be widely read and studied today. Some of her most famous essays include "A Room of One's Own," in which she argues for the importance of women's financial independence in order to achieve creative freedom, and "Three Guineas," which explores the connections between gender inequality and war.

Woolf struggled with mental illness throughout her life, and in 1941 she committed suicide by drowning herself in the River Ouse near her home in Sussex. Her legacy as a pioneering writer and feminist thinker has continued to grow in the decades since her death, and she is now widely recognized as one of the most important literary figures of the 20th century.
